Testimonio de Miguel

A family's experience with cultural disconnect in therapy

Watch the video at the top of your module for Miguel's full story.

When I first brought my son Miguel to therapy, I had hoped it would be a healing space—a place where he could feel understood and supported. Instead, I found myself facing a painful encounter that left me feeling dismissed and misunderstood.

"Do you think your parenting style might be contributing to Miguel's anxiety?"

The way she spoke suggested that our approach as a family—with its emphasis on close-knit relationships, respect for elders, and emotional expressiveness—was something to be examined rather than understood.

The breaking point came when she suggested Miguel might benefit from "more independence"—a concept that felt entirely foreign to our family's values. In our culture, family support isn't a sign of weakness or over-dependence. It's a source of strength, a beautiful web of relationships that sustains us through difficult times.

What Dr. Thompson failed to understand was that cultural context isn't just background noise. It's the melody of our lives, the rhythm that gives meaning to our experiences.

Vea el video al inicio de su módulo para la historia completa de Miguel.

Cuando llevé a mi hijo Miguel a terapia por primera vez, lo hice con la esperanza de que encontráramos un lugar donde él pudiera sentirse escuchado, acompañado y comprendido.

"¿Cree que su estilo de crianza puede estar causando la ansiedad de Miguel?"

Para ella, parecía que el hecho de que seamos una familia muy unida, que valoremos el respeto por los mayores y que expresemos nuestras emociones abiertamente, era algo que debía analizarse o corregirse, en lugar de entenderse.

Lo que la Dra. Thompson no supo ver es que nuestra cultura no es un detalle menor ni un simple fondo. Es el corazón de nuestra historia, el ritmo que le da sentido a lo que somos.

Cultural Frameworks

Humility · Responsiveness · Sustainment

Cultural Humility Lifelong Process

A lifelong process of self-critique and self-awareness wherein providers recognize their own cultural biases and power dynamics inherent in professional relationships. It involves an ongoing commitment to learning from clients and communities while acknowledging that cultural knowledge is never complete or static.

Self-awareness & reflection Recognition of power imbalances Commitment to lifelong learning Client-centered approach

Independence: A Case Study

PerspectiveView of Independence
PractitionerPositive developmental skill children should develop early
Family (interdependent culture)Values interdependence; independence may signal problems or depression

Cultural humility invites us to explore why we hold these views—not to judge one as superior.

Cultural Responsiveness Adaptive Practice

The ability to adapt practices, policies, and services to align with the cultural beliefs, values, and practices of the individuals and communities being served. Cultural responsiveness involves modifying existing interventions and approaches to better fit cultural contexts while maintaining program effectiveness.

Adaptation of existing practices Integration of cultural values Flexible implementation Culturally informed assessment

Cultural Sustainment Systemic Change

The systematic integration and preservation of cultural practices, values, and worldviews within service delivery systems. Cultural sustainment goes beyond adaptation to ensure that cultural elements are not merely added on but are fundamentally woven into the fabric of programs and maintained over time.

Long-term integration Preservation of traditional practices Community ownership & leadership Structural changes

Cultural Humility Action Steps

Phase 1: Acknowledge & Explore — Listen First ▼

DO Ask families to explain their understanding of independence. Use open-ended questions. Allow time for full explanation.

AVOID Making assumptions about resistance. Dismissing family values. Rushing to solutions.

Phase 1: Acknowledge & Explore — Clarify Values ▼

DO Explore family's cultural framework. Ask about interdependence practices. Understand family structure and roles.

AVOID Assuming one-size-fits-all definitions. Interpreting differences as deficits.

Phase 2: Find Common Ground — Shared Goals ▼

DO Look for underlying skills both value. Discuss self-advocacy, confidence, problem-solving. Focus on child's wellbeing.

AVOID Forcing practitioner agenda. Ignoring family priorities.

Phase 3: Bridge Perspectives — Reframe Goals ▼

DO Present skills as enhancing family contribution. Show how abilities support interdependence. Connect to family values.

AVOID Abandoning professional knowledge. Creating false dichotomies.

Ongoing Practice: Self-Reflection & Power Awareness ▼

DO Examine your bias toward independence. Acknowledge professional privilege. Recognize pressure on families. Share decision-making.

AVOID Assuming cultural neutrality. Becoming defensive. Minimizing power dynamics.

Mindful Intervention Planning

Scenario-based practice with mindfulness integration

Scenario

You are an early intervention provider working on an intervention plan with a bilingual family who lives in the U.S. Monday–Friday and travels to Mexico every weekend to spend time with their family. This family comes from a different racial and ethnic background than yourself. You are working quickly as this family has been waiting for intervention for 9 months already.

Reflection Questions for the Plan

  • What privileges do I hold as a coach, and how are these different from those of the family?
  • How can I sustain practices that respect and honor the family's cultural values?
  • What values does the family prioritize in this case?
  • How do my own values and experiences influence the way I view this family's situation?
  • What values are being prioritized in the intervention I am using?
  • Where might there be tension between the intervention's priorities and the family's priorities?
  • What strategies can I use to bridge differences between intervention values and family values?
